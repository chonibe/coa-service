# Artist Bios — Rewritten

---

**Jérôme Masi** — Annecy, France
Jérôme Masi studied at Emile-Cohl in Lyon, worked through game studios and motion design, and eventually landed in Annecy as a full-time illustrator. His commercial work goes to airlines, watch brands, and telecoms; his personal paintings go on canvas with acrylic and quiet. The palette stays deliberately sober. He talks about wanting to leave room in each image for the viewer to bring their own memory to it, which sounds modest until you actually spend time with one.

---

**Moritz Adam Schmitt** — Cologne, Germany
Moritz Adam Schmitt spent a year posting one icon a day to Instagram. That discipline turned into an invitation to Adobe MAX, then into a freelance career with clients like Adobe, Microsoft, Xbox, Porsche, and Samsung. His process starts with iPad sketches and ends in tight vector work — bold outlines, flat color, the attitude of a good street poster without any of the mess.

---

**Loreta Isac** — Bucharest, Romania
Loreta Isac grew up in Iași, studied art there, and moved to Bucharest. She describes herself as "once upon a dreamer," which captures the tone of the work exactly. Her illustrations and animations move between editorial commissions for the New York Times and Washington Post and quieter personal pieces — frozen moments, unnamed feelings, figures caught mid-thought. She uses motion as just another way to let the drawing breathe.

---

**Marc David Spengler** — Stuttgart, Germany
Marc David Spengler graduated communication design from Stuttgart's state academy in 2022 and immediately started working with clients who care about craft: Hermès, The New Yorker, Google, Vans, The Atlantic. The work moves between hand media and screen without losing its texture — ink that still feels considered even when the final file is digital. He is represented in North America and China.

---

**Dawal** — Paris, France
Dawal started painting the streets of Paris around 18. His thing is working with what the city already gives him — peeling plaster, cracked walls, odd shapes in aging surfaces — and placing miniature surreal scenes inside them. Images that stop you mid-pavement. He runs an open studio in the 18th arrondissement and has had solo shows at Galerie Artistik Rezo, where the same instinct moves from wall to canvas without losing its intimacy.

---

**Antonia Lev** — Paris, France
Antonia Lev has been working as an illustrator and street artist since at least 2011. Her practice moves between wall painting, editorial image-making, and digital work without settling firmly in any of them. The images are graphic and narrative — figures and scenes that hold mural scale and still reward a close look. She painted through lockdowns and festival summers alike, always asking how much story fits on the surface in front of her.

---

**Hedof** — Breda, Netherlands
Rick Berkelmans runs Hedof as a one-person studio where drawing and printmaking stay close. Ink gets scanned, layered digitally, and sometimes returned to paper in limited runs. Animals, pattern, and pop culture wink at you from work made for Nike, IKEA, The New York Times, and Google. The work is genuinely warm and graphically sharp at the same time, which is harder than it looks.

---

**Taloosh** — Haifa, Israel
Taloosh Studio works from Haifa on promo art, illustration, and creative campaigns. The reel moves fast — music, culture, commercial briefs treated like short films. Coastal confidence is built into everything they make.

---

**Yonil** — Tel Aviv, Israel
Jonathan Lax has been working as YONIL from Tel Aviv long enough to land in illustration annuals like IdN and Illustration Now!. The work comes from gig-poster culture — typographically bold, music-adjacent, loud enough to read from the back of a room. Commercial work and personal pieces share the same slightly anxious humor. Graphic, but never cold.

---

**Troy Browne** — Nottingham, UK
Troy Browne spent years in motion design studios before going independent in 2018. The studio background left him with technique and restlessness — collage sensibility, hand-drawn frame logic, digital finishing that still feels assembled rather than generated. His print shop work makes clear he cares about the object as much as the timeline.

---

**Elfassi** — Israel
Elfassi graduated Shenkar in visual communication and never picked just one surface. Embroidery, mural paint, digital line, and music posters all come from the same hand and the same graphic sensibility. The materials change; the voice stays consistent.

---

**Emilio Cerezo** — Spain
Emilio Cerezo grew up in Murcia, studied fine arts in Granada, and ran under graffiti aliases until 2012 when he started putting his own name on walls. The academic training paid off in specific ways — figure, landscape, and mural scale now share a painterly attention to mood over representation. He wants people to experience his murals the way they experience landscape or music: not as a message, but as an atmosphere. He still tattoos between murals, which probably explains the line quality.

---

**Igal Talianski** — Haifa, Israel
Igal Talianski works in collage and mixed media from Haifa, stacking paper, print, and found color into surfaces that feel assembled and intentional at the same time. Climbing brands and editorial clients respond to the tactile honesty of it. On a wall or in a frame, the invitation is always the same: step closer and notice what was kept.

---

**Carsten Gueth** — Stuttgart, Germany
Carsten Gueth studied architecture and urban planning in Stuttgart before spending fifteen years in graphic design for cultural institutions — museums, festivals, publishers. His independent practice publishes as Die Doing. The work is bold, occasionally absurd, and built to read from across a room. Recent recognition from ADC, Red Dot, and Palau de la Música Catalana suggests the pivot was the right call.

---

**Or Bar-El** — Israel
Or Bar-El's SketchBoom project puts a camera on the drawing hand and broadcasts the act of making live. Bezalel animation training and a decorated student film set the foundation; independent practice pulled him back toward illustration and walls. The common thread is immediacy — drawing as an event you witness, not just an object you receive.

---

**Ori Toor** — Tel Aviv, Israel
Ori Toor improvises. His drawings accumulate line by line, each one suggesting the next, until the surface is full of creatures, patterns, and color that refuses to stay put. He studied at Shenkar, worked newspaper layout for a while, and ended up making commercial work for Nike, Wired, and Cartoon Network alongside a personal practice built around what he calls "Gibberish" drawings — improvisational, meditative, the opposite of planned. His work appeared on Adobe Illustrator's splash screen in 2018. People describe it like music you can see.

---

**My Sunbeam** — London, UK
Taylor works as My Sunbeam and makes illustration that feels borrowed from sticker books and late-night radio — characters with an odd, immediately likable warmth and textures distressed just enough to give them age. Popular art in the best sense: made to be collected without ceremony.

---

**Linda Baritski** — London, UK
Linda Baritski spent years inside major studios before SEASONOFVICTORY became her public name. The independent practice landed quickly — a Kiehl's global campaign across 48 locations in 2022–23 was an early sign. The work is color-committed and character-forward, shaped by a background in art direction that knows exactly how to make things land, whether on packaging or a wall.

---

**Erezoo** — Haifa, Israel
Erez Sameach works as Erezoo from Haifa, where illustration, graphic design, and graffiti are treated as the same practice rather than three separate disciplines. Born in Carmiel, trained at Wizo, he helped build the SIRA collective alongside relatives who paint the same streets. The output is loud, coastal, and rooted in the city's cultural calendar.

---

**Maalavidaa** — Montreal, Canada
Alycia Rainaud built Maalavidaa around a 2018 thesis on the role of psychology in design. The neon gradients and saturated abstraction aren't decorative — they're the argument: color as a tool for emotional regulation, rhythm as something you can actually feel. The work doesn't tell you how to feel. It gives you something to feel with.

---

**Psoman** — Liège, Belgium
Psoman studied graphic design in Belgium and welding and polyester fabrication in Switzerland before committing to painting full time. Hybrid animals and surreal bodies carry quiet warnings about nature under pressure across walls in Europe and Asia. A school residency in Taiwan in 2024 proved the characters travel without losing anything. The STRAAT Museum in Amsterdam holds one of his pieces.

---

**Agus Rucula** — Argentina / Italy
Agus Rucula paints the human figure at mural scale and treats it as a meeting point — between community and artist, between dance and paint, between the people who made the wall and the people walking past it. Her practice draws as much from photography and choreography as from urban art tradition. Communities often participate directly. The result is empathy at the size of architecture.

---

**Max Diamond**
Max Diamond makes posters that understand fandom from the inside. Each one leads with a single clean idea — the visual logic of an album cover applied to characters and moments people already carry in their heads. Meant to read across a room and reward a closer look.

---

**Ezra Baderman** — Lisbon, Portugal
Ezra Baderman was born in Paris, grew up in London, trained at John Cass and Bezalel, and now works in Lisbon. His practice — collage, painting, UV work, salvaged street posters from Tel Aviv walls — is shaped by all of that movement. Fragments of political imagery get rearranged into something quieter and stranger. The street is both material and argument.

---

**Marylou Faure** — London, UK
Marylou Faure studied art direction in Paris and moved to London in 2012. She built a practice around figures who stare back without eyes yet feel completely present. The work is deliberately cheeky — saturated color, poster-graphic bones, characters who own their space. She has made solo shows and brand campaigns for McLaren F1 and Kiehl's without softening either end.

---

**Dima Korma** — Berlin, Germany
Dima Korma is a Berlin-based muralist, originally from Israel, who studied visual communication at Shenkar. His walls layer paste, paint, and type fragments over the existing character of a building. Abstract, but not empty — each surface feels like it's describing a climate the space was already hinting at.

---

**Samme Snow** — London, UK
Samme Snow finished a Graphic Communication degree in Farnham in 2013 and chose walls over office work. He has painted corporate lobbies, skate spaces, and magazine editorial spreads with the same generous, unhurried line. Since 2020 he has run open coloring workshops with thousands of participants, which feels continuous with the mural practice rather than separate from it.

---

**Sancho Sancho** — Amsterdam, Netherlands
Sancho Sancho is a visual artist and illustrator who grew up in Birmingham and Shanghai before settling in Amsterdam. The characters are quirky, the color bold, the hand-drawn line consistent across personal prints and commercial work for brands like NBCUniversal, LG, and Carlsberg. Humor runs just underneath everything.

---

**Alice Bureau** — Zurich, Switzerland
Alice Hoffmann spent two decades in agency creative leadership before Bureau Alice became her primary output. The transition shows in the confidence — a line clean enough for global brand systems (Meta, Spotify, Swiss Post stamps) that still feels hand-made at its core. Editorial, packaging, and the occasional wall all come from the same place: a question about how little line can carry how much story.

---

**Alin Mor** — Haifa, Israel
Alin Mor graduated Bezalel in visual communications and built a practice around femininity, archetype, and spiritual symbolism at mural scale. The work asks, without quite saying it, who gets to occupy public space. It answers with saturated, symbolic figures that are hard to walk past without stopping.

---

**Beto Val** — Ecuador
Beto Valencia makes digital collages from vintage scientific illustration — birds, bones, botanical plates — and arranges them into ecosystems where nothing quite follows the original logic. The pandemic years turned a personal practice into something with gallery presence, museum listings, and a steady print shop. Every piece feels like a cabinet of curiosities tipped sideways.

---

**Cokorda Martin** — Bali, Indonesia
Cokorda Martin grew up in Klungkung inside a family of makers. Traditional Balinese motifs — ritual pattern, temple color, mythic reference — are his raw material, but he doesn't treat them as historical objects. They show up in album art, brand collaborations with Café Kitsuné and Uniqlo, and editorial work for international clients. Island craft meeting global briefs, without losing anything in the translation.

---

**Aviv Shamir** — Tel Aviv, Israel
Aviv Shamir is a Tel Aviv illustrator who keeps the process visible. Sketches, finished pieces, and mid-thought experiments share the same feed — faces, humor, and the texture of daily city life. The transparency is part of the appeal: you see the hand, the hesitation, and the result in one scroll.

---

**Hen Macabi** — Israel
Hen Macabi is an Israeli illustrator and art director whose typography-first work has shaped some of the country's most recognizable pop moments. His cover for Netta Barzilai's "Bassa Sababa" — released in the lead-up to her Eurovision win — is the most visible example. He trained at Shenkar. The AlefAlefAlef project, a graphic tribute to 70 years of Israeli cinema done in VHS-cover logic, shows what he does with more room.

---

**Refiloe Mnisi** — Johannesburg, South Africa
Refiloe Mnisi is a Johannesburg illustrator whose figures arrive dressed with intention. The work sits at the intersection of streetwear culture and portrait confidence — each character feels assembled on purpose, with attitude to match. The voice is sharp and unapologetically stylish.

---

**Geometric Bang** — Florence, Italy
Mattia Botta has been working as Geometric Bang since the late 1990s, starting in graffiti and moving steadily toward a practice that now spans murals, illustration, tattooing, and canvas. Born in Lodi, based in Florence, he works with pencil, spray, and ink across a graphic vocabulary built on bold color blocks with human warmth underneath. European festivals and Asian walls share space on his calendar.

---

**Laura Fridman** — Tel Aviv, Israel
Laura Fridman is French, studied economics at Yale, danced with the Israel Ballet, and then started painting. Her figurative work exaggerates hands and necks while keeping faces photographic — a tension that feels physical, like the body is working through something. She has shown in Tel Aviv, Paris, and Barcelona.

---

**Cubi Boumclap** — Agen, France
Cubi Boumclap works from southwestern France as an illustrator, comic artist, and designer who treats the screen and the alley as close cousins. The line is fast, direct, and honest about commerce — open for studios, publishers, and anyone who needs graphics with street timing. His early years were shaped by the La Streetarterie collective; the work still carries that energy.

---

**Studio Giftig** — Netherlands
Studio Giftig is Niels van Swaemen and Kaspar van Leek, two Dutch painters who have been working together since 2007 and painting murals full time since 2012. The work is surreal realism at building scale — cinematic light, careful staging, finished surfaces that make you wonder how human hands did it. They photograph their own reference material, which keeps everything under their control and recognizably theirs.

---

**Unapaulogetic** — Tel Aviv, Israel
Paul Rozenboim publishes as Unapaulogetic — a Tel Aviv multidisciplinary practice fluent in motion, typography, illustration, and installation. Projects chase physics through software: pendulums, generative systems, typographic objects frozen mid-spin. Limited editions compress the same energy into pieces made for real rooms.

---

**Thales Towers** — Tel Aviv, Israel
Thales Towers says on his own site that he doesn't know how to paint faces, so he paints towers on them instead. His Tel Aviv street pieces feature tower-headed characters landing punchlines for pedestrians. Animated GIFs spread the same cast worldwide on GIPHY. The humor is consistent and the drawing backs it up.

---

**Nia Shtai** — Tel Aviv, Israel
Nia Shtai is a Tel Aviv illustrator and graphic designer working across Procreate and screen-based tools. Characters, fashion, and a certain dream logic share the same saturated color space. She also runs an upcycled fashion brand, which makes sense — the same care about image and material runs through both.

---

**Nurit Gross** — Tel Aviv, Israel
Nurit Gross is a Tel Aviv illustrator who graduated Bezalel in 2019 and works across cut paper, gouache, markers, and digital paint like they're all in the same drawer. Museums, magazines, municipal projects, and children's books share the same curiosity about folk pattern and humor. She won the Israel Museum's Ben-Yitzhak Award in 2022 and has been included in Society of Illustrators annuals.

---

**Moshe Gilboa** — Israel
Moshe Gilboa is an Israeli artist and designer whose practice doesn't file neatly. Animation, UI, indie games, and illustrated essays share the same humor — work that winks while it teaches. He documents shipped projects in long-form on Medium, which tells you something about how seriously he takes the process of making.

---

**Eden Kalif** — Tel Aviv, Israel
Eden Kalif is an illustrator and tattoo artist who graduated Bezalel and kept both practices going in parallel. His line does the heavy lifting — faces, hands, and symbols with almost nothing extra. Clean, precise, and consistent whether the surface is paper, skin, or a print run.

---

**Tiffany Chin** — Toronto, Canada
Tiffany Chin studied illustration at OCAD University in Toronto and graduated in 2022 with a medal and a stack of award wins — Society of Illustrators, Applied Arts, Communication Arts, Creative Quarterly. The work is inspired by 1960s and 70s poster culture and carries that era's typographic confidence into contemporary illustration. She has been shortlisted or recognized every year since graduation.

---

**Tiago Hesp** — Portugal
Tiago Hesp was born in 1981, trained in scenography at Escola Superior de Teatro e Cinema in Lisbon, and has been doing graffiti since 1998. His practice now spans mural painting, music, illustration, and theatre design — disciplines that share a grammar of stage and surface. He describes painting as starting from an aggressive black line and chasing balance between shadow and color.

---

**Yoaz** — Paris, France
Yoaz is a Paris illustrator whose compositions feel like crowded rooms where every element is competing for attention and somehow harmony wins. Street art appetite and advertising discipline share the same restless DNA across murals, merch, and brand campaigns. Hi-Fructose covered the work in 2019 and called it abuzz with activity — accurate for a practice built on controlled visual noise.

---

**TWOONE** — Italy / Australia
Hiroyasu Tsuri left Yokohama for Melbourne in 2003 and spent years absorbing street and graffiti culture before formalizing his practice. He coined the term "Raw Mark Making" to describe a style that runs classical drawing through street velocity. Represented by Backwoods Gallery in Melbourne, he has shown in institutions in Canberra, Amsterdam, and Munich, and his work is in museum collections. Graffiti grammar holding museum weight.

---

**S A R G O N**
The @oky.sargon account is a studio diary kept in public — illustration and motion posted as it's finished. The feed is the statement: style, pace, and curiosity on display in real time.

---

**Kymo One** — Ghent, Belgium
Kymo One is a Belgian muralist trained in graphic illustration who never gave up spray paint. Chains, roses, smoke, and industrial romanticism repeat across European walls — old-master drama at graffiti velocity. Readable from the sidewalk and worth getting closer to.

---

**Tyler Shelton** — Texas, USA
Tyler Shelton started painting portraits of rock stars at 20 and turned his Instagram into a business before he was 30. The practice moved from music iconography toward a broader pop culture range — thick acrylic, stencil confidence, celebrity portraits that treat fame as a material like any other. Bright, cheeky, and backed by real craft.

---

**saturn_png** — France
Hermann works as saturn_png, pairing hard-edge abstraction with something that echoes ancient ceramic and Greek pottery. Procreate grain and analog discipline come together in illustrations that feel carved as much as drawn. The work asks how digital art can feel archaeological.

---

**Levi Jacobs** — Rotterdam, Netherlands
Levi Jacobs is a Rotterdam illustrator who treats every commission like a film still — light, cast, and prop chosen deliberately. He studied illustration at St. Joost and has been freelancing since 2010. The work is slightly noir, slightly surreal, and consistently human.

---

**Jack AC Art**
Jack AC Art builds a small universe where illustration meets greeting-card wit. The tone is intimate — phrases you'd send a friend, pictures you'd hang in a kitchen. Made to be held, mailed, and kept.

---

**Wuper Kec** — Serbia
Wuper Kec is a Serbian muralist who has been doing graffiti since 2006 and large-scale brush work for over five years. Wolves, birds, and human hybrids hold walls across Europe. Fairy tale on first look, something stranger on the second.

---

**Tania Yakunova** — London, UK
Tania Yakunova was born and raised in Kyiv, worked in the creative industry for years, and became a full-time illustrator in 2014. She relocated to London in 2023. The work crosses commercial illustration and fine art — geometry, texture, and literary reference sharing the same table. Ceramic work sits alongside the print and digital output. Her best work, she says, comes from merging different realms, which the practice clearly does.

---

**Ajax Blyth Piper** — Hastings, UK
Ajax Blyth Piper graduated from University of the Arts London and has been working as a muralist on England's south coast since 2018. His pieces read like narrative snapshots — figures you might know, palettes tuned to the neighborhood they live in. He also designs typefaces, and the same attention to letterform runs through the mural work.

---

**Raki** — Philippines
RAF Page works as RAKI, an illustrator whose large pieces feel like storybook spreads scaled to architecture. The palette is warm and the characters slightly toylike — mythic and accessible at the same time. Active in Bangkok and Southeast Asian illustration festivals.

---

**ODSK** — Nantes, France
Anthony Daudet works as ODSK from Nantes, where tattooing and illustration inform each other. Risograph prints, merch, and mural-scale work share the same decisive line — flash sheet energy applied with editorial patience.

---

**Kaká Chazz** — Minas Gerais, Brazil
Cassiano Henrique Cândido, known as Kaká Chazz, has been painting walls since 2008 in and around Três Pontas in southern Brazil. His murals translate everyday Minas scenes — work, faith, family — into something monumental that neighbors actually recognize. The style bridges classical figure drawing and graffiti scale without forcing them to pretend they're the same thing.

---

**Chubby Nida** — Bangkok, Thailand
Chubby Nida is a Bangkok illustrator whose round, gentle figures carry social commentary without announcing it. Editorial and personal pieces share the same approach: approachable shapes, colors you want to keep, ideas that land after the smile. She is part of a generation redefining what "cute" can argue for in public.

---

**Lobster Robin** — Belgium
Lobster Robin is a Belgian painter and street artist with a background in illustration and animation. The characters feel like graphic novels escaped onto brick — saturated outlines, narrative freeze-frames, enough warmth on the surface to bring in a wide audience and enough timing underneath to hold them.

---

**Crackthetoy** — Indonesia
Crack works as Crackthetoy — a graffiti artist, toy designer, and digital illustrator based in Indonesia. The characters are collectible and slightly mischievous, living between product fantasy and mural scale. Bold outlines, candy color, stories you read in one glance.

---

**Mameko Maeda** — Tokyo, Japan
Mameko Maeda is a Tokyo illustrator born in 1993 who turned professional in 2020. Her figures carry gentle humor and precise ink — editorial and personal projects sharing an affection for youth culture, fashion, and quiet punchlines. She makes cute feel smart and smart feel approachable.

---

**Frederique Mati** — Amsterdam, Netherlands
Frederique Matti is a Dutch illustrator and art director who has been freelancing for over ten years. Editorial, branding, and personal prints share a restrained palette that knows exactly when to open up. Her characters live in the same city she does — bikes, rainlight, calm confidence. She has worked with Google, Uber, Spotify, and GitHub, and held a solo painting show at The Hoxton Amsterdam in 2023.

---

**Phil Huelz** — Cologne, Germany
Phil Huelz is a Cologne illustrator who treats every brief as a conversation. Editorial, advertising, and personal work share bold color blocking and faces you believe. The thinking is visible — ideas wrapped in drawing that stays accessible without dumbing anything down.

---

**Ollie Smither** — Devon, UK
Ollie Smither is a UK illustrator based in Devon who works in fine line. Forests, figures, and small impossible details accumulate in drawings you discover gradually. The mood is contemplative and slightly uncanny — work for people who like to lean in.

---

**NASCA Uno** — Berlin, Germany
Nasca Uno paints Berlin walls with the patience of a draftsman and the nerve of a graffiti writer. Myth, anatomy, and urban lore stack into murals that have found their way into museum collections, including the STRAAT Museum in Amsterdam. Each piece reads like a chapter — figures from a century you can't quite place.

---

**Animalitoland** — Los Angeles, USA
Graciela Gonçalves Da Silva works as Animalitoland — a name that sounds like a theme park and paints like one. She studied design at the University of Buenos Aires, started painting murals in 2011, and has been working nomadically since 2014, living across Buenos Aires, Madrid, Vancouver, and now Los Angeles. The acrylic worlds read like graphic novels pressed into architecture — precise characters, genuine strangeness, a cast that travels well.

---

**Thibaud Hérem** — France / UK
Thibaud Hérem is a French illustrator known for hand-drawn architectural work in pencil and Indian ink. The buildings feel walked, not photographed — facades rendered with an obsessive patience that turns a structure into a portrait. He has worked for Hermès, Netflix (Our Beloved Summer), and Four Seasons, published books with Nobrow and Laurence King, and held a solo show in Seoul.

---

**Jennypo Art**
Jenny Pokryvailo is a tattooist and illustrator whose line never forgets skin. Whether the surface is body or paper, the drawing stays decisive — ornament, narrative, and negative space in careful balance. Fans follow for the same reason clients book: every mark earns its place.

---

**Mathew Gagnon** — Montreal, Canada
Matthew Gagnon is a Montreal artist who uses walls and panels as stages for symbolic narrative. Figures, animals, and pattern stack into scenes that feel borrowed from dreams and collective memory. The palette is bold, the drawing disciplined — the same storyteller working at street scale and gallery scale.

---

**Rik Lee** — Melbourne, Australia
Rik Lee is a Melbourne illustrator who mixes tattoo flash, fashion editorial, and diary drawing into something that holds together. Women, wolves, and wry slogans repeat like favorite records — familiar in structure, specific in feeling. Retro in palette without being in costume.

---

**Paola Delfín** — Mexico City, Mexico
Paola Delfín is a Mexican muralist whose large-scale women seem to grow from the wall itself. Black, white, and soft gray carry the emotion without raising their voice — curves that echo Art Nouveau and street timing in the same breath. Her figures have appeared in cities across the Americas and Europe, and each one feels like it belongs to the neighborhood it lives in.

---

**Wotto** — Orange County, USA
Craig Watkins is Wotto — a British illustrator based in Orange County who has been freelancing since graduating with a BA in Illustration and Design in 2000. The practice has covered toys, apparel, advertising, and personal character universes in the years since. UK wit, California color, pop surrealism for people who read the label.

---

**Yippie Hey** — Hamburg, Germany
YIPPIEHEY is a Hamburg illustration and animation studio founded by Jacob Eisinger. The client list is wide — Adidas, Samsung, McDonald's, Adobe, Deutsche Bahn, UNICEF — and so are the editorial credits: Wired, The New York Times, Der Spiegel. Friendly character design without being saccharine.

---

**Keya Tama** — New York, USA
Keya Tama was born in Cape Town in 1997 and now works from New York. Her practice braids ancestral symbolism with graphic minimalism — each piece asking how much you can strip away and still say something complete. She has shown with Hashimoto Contemporary in New York and Subliminal Projects in Los Angeles, painted murals at festivals and institutions across Africa and Europe, and collaborated with the Royal Delft Museum on ceramic work.

---

**Facio** — Argentina / Portugal
Tomás Facio was born in 1990 in Merlo, studied at the National University of Arts in Buenos Aires from 2013, and paints walls across Argentina and beyond. Figures, typography, and local symbols interlock in murals that read from a block away and hold up closer. A designer's attention to silhouette, a muralist's appetite for scale.

---

**Woizo** — Toulouse, France
Woizo is a painter and muralist based in Toulouse who fills walls with narrative — creatures standing in for human impulses, better and worse. Graphic clarity keeps the story readable from across the street; detail rewards getting closer. Humor, unease, and genuine beauty in the same frame.

---

**Thomas Stary** — Prague, Czech Republic
Tomáš Starý is a Prague designer who trained in industrial design at the University of West Bohemia and has been painting murals since 2010. His walls treat the city like a typographic poster — geometry, rhythm, and the kind of balance that industrial design training quietly enforces. The same logic runs through illustration work and architectural-scale pieces alike.

---

**Vivaladybug**
Viva La Ladybug is an illustrator whose world has the energy of a friendly arcade — bold outlines, cheeky mascots, characters built for movement even when they're standing still. Instagram and GIPHY carry the same loopable joy. Fans use the work like punctuation in conversation, which is a good sign.

---

**Iain Macarthur** — London, UK
Iain Macarthur is a London illustrator who builds portraits out of filigree — faces that dissolve into wildlife, geometry, and surreal detail. He grew up in Swindon, studied at Swindon College, and has been freelancing from London since 2009. Music packaging, apparel, and gallery work share the same marathon patience. The reward is density you can actually get lost in.

---

**Igor Mikutski**
Igor Mikutski is a painter who treats social media video the way older generations used sketchbooks — brush tests, half-finished canvases, process posted as it happens. The work is large-scale, figurative, and acrylic-on-paper. The finished pieces carry the pulse of being made in public.
